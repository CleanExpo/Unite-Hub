# How Long Does Water Damage Drying Really Take? (Commercial Guide)

The most common question property managers ask after a flood or pipe burst: "How long until it's dry?" The honest answer is: it depends on at least six variables, and anyone giving you a blanket number without measuring the site is guessing.

Here's a realistic breakdown of what affects commercial drying timelines, what the IICRC S500 standard says, and when a job needs escalation.

---

## What Factors Affect Drying Time?

### 1. Volume of Water and Saturation Depth

Surface water on sealed concrete dries in 2-3 days with proper equipment. Water that has saturated carpet, underlay, plasterboard, timber framing, or insulation takes significantly longer — because you're not just evaporating surface moisture, you're drawing trapped moisture out of porous materials.

A Category 1 (clean water) pipe burst on a tiled commercial floor might dry in 48-72 hours. The same volume of water on carpeted offices with plasterboard walls could take 5-7 days or longer.

### 2. Ambient Conditions

Brisbane's subtropical humidity is the single biggest factor that extends drying times compared to drier climates. When outdoor relative humidity sits at 70-80% (common from October to April), dehumidifiers must work harder to create the moisture gradient that drives evaporation.

Temperature matters too. Warmer air holds more moisture, which accelerates evaporation from wet materials — but only if the dehumidifier can keep up with the additional moisture load. The sweet spot for refrigerant dehumidifiers is 20-30C, which is typical for air-conditioned commercial spaces.

### 3. Material Types

Different materials absorb and release moisture at very different rates:

- **Concrete (sealed):** Surface dries in 1-2 days; deep slab moisture can take weeks
- **Concrete (unsealed/polished):** 3-7 days depending on slab thickness
- **Timber flooring:** 4-7 days minimum; may require subfloor drying
- **Plasterboard (gyprock):** 3-5 days if caught early; replacement required if saturated above 300mm wicking height
- **Carpet and underlay:** 2-4 days for carpet; underlay often requires removal
- **Insulation (wall cavity):** Typically requires removal rather than drying — fibreglass and cellulose insulation rarely dries effectively in situ

### 4. Equipment Deployed

Underpowered equipment is the number one cause of extended drying times. The IICRC S500 standard provides psychrometric calculations for determining the correct number and type of dehumidifiers and air movers based on the specific conditions of each job.

As a rough guide for commercial spaces:
- **Dehumidifiers:** One 70L/day unit per 50-80m2 of affected area (adjusted for ambient conditions)
- **Air movers:** One per 4-5m2 of wet surface area
- **Desiccant units:** Required for wall cavity and subfloor drying where refrigerant units can't reach

### 5. Water Category

The IICRC S500 classifies water damage into three categories:

- **Category 1 (Clean water):** Broken supply lines, rainwater through intact roof — standard drying protocols
- **Category 2 (Grey water):** Dishwasher/washing machine overflow, toilet overflow (urine only), HVAC condensate — requires antimicrobial treatment and potentially more aggressive material removal
- **Category 3 (Black water):** Sewage, floodwater, rising water from rivers/creeks — requires removal of all porous materials that contacted the water, antimicrobial treatment, and extended drying with contamination controls

Category 3 water damage always takes longer because the remediation scope is larger — you're removing and replacing materials, not just drying them.

### 6. Building Access and HVAC

Commercial buildings with functional HVAC systems dry faster because you can control temperature and supplement air circulation. Buildings with compromised HVAC (common after flooding) or limited access (tenanted spaces, operating businesses) require creative equipment placement and often take 20-30% longer.

## What Does the IICRC S500 Standard Say?

The IICRC S500 doesn't prescribe a specific number of days for drying. Instead, it defines completion criteria based on moisture content:

- **Drying is complete** when affected materials reach their normal moisture content (equilibrium moisture content, or EMC) relative to similar unaffected materials in the same structure
- Moisture readings must be taken with calibrated meters — pin-type for timber, non-invasive for plasterboard and concrete
- **Documentation** of moisture readings throughout the drying process is mandatory for insurance purposes

In practice, this means:
- **Timber:** Target MC of 12-16% (varies by species and climate zone)
- **Plasterboard:** Target MC matching unaffected sections, typically below 1% on a non-invasive meter
- **Concrete:** Target readings matching unaffected slab sections

The standard explicitly warns against relying on "touch" or visual assessment to determine dryness. Materials can feel dry to the touch while retaining dangerous levels of internal moisture.

## Realistic Commercial Drying Timelines

| Scenario | Typical Duration |
|----------|-----------------|
| Small office, Category 1, hard floors | 2-3 days |
| Medium office, Category 1, carpet | 4-6 days |
| Large commercial, Category 1, mixed materials | 5-8 days |
| Any space, Category 2 | Add 1-2 days for remediation steps |
| Any space, Category 3 | 7-14+ days (includes material removal and rebuild) |
| Subfloor/cavity drying | 5-10 days with desiccant units |
| Concrete slab (deep saturation) | 2-4 weeks with specialist equipment |

These are indicative ranges assuming correct equipment deployment from day one. Delays in starting the drying process — even 24-48 hours — can double the total timeline and significantly increase the risk of mould growth.

## When to Escalate

Contact a specialist restorer (not just an equipment hire company) if:

- **Mould is visible** or you detect musty odours — this indicates drying has already failed and remediation (IICRC S520) is required
- **Moisture readings are not trending downward** after 48 hours of drying — equipment may be undersized or there's an undetected moisture source
- **Category 3 water is involved** — this is not a DIY job under any circumstances
- **The structure contains asbestos-containing materials** — water damage to ACM requires licensed asbestos assessment before any disturbance
- **Insurance is involved** — professional documentation from the outset protects your claim
