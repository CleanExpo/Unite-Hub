# ATO Audit Triggers in 2026: What Australian Businesses Need to Know

The ATO completed over 1.5 million compliance interventions in 2024-25, recovering $18.2 billion in tax liabilities. Their data-matching programme now ingests information from banks, cryptocurrency exchanges, share registries, property titles offices, state revenue offices, and international tax authorities under the Common Reporting Standard (CRS).

If your business has compliance gaps, the ATO will find them — usually before you do. Here are the top triggers drawing ATO attention in 2026 and what you can do about each one.

## 1. Lifestyle and Income Mismatch

The ATO's data-matching programme cross-references your declared income against property purchases, vehicle registrations, overseas travel records, and luxury goods purchases. If you're declaring $80,000 in business income but purchasing a $1.2 million property — expect questions.

**What to do:** Ensure all income sources are declared, including capital gains, foreign income, and trust distributions. If you've received a one-off windfall (inheritance, asset sale), document the source clearly.

## 2. Excessive Work-Related Deductions

The ATO publishes occupation-specific benchmarks for work-related deductions. Claims significantly above your occupation's norm are automatically flagged. Common over-claims include:

- Home office expenses claimed at flat rate without actual usage calculation
- Vehicle expenses using the logbook method without a valid logbook
- Self-education expenses with no clear connection to current employment
- Tools and equipment claimed in full rather than depreciated

**What to do:** Keep receipts for every deduction. If claiming home office, maintain a diary of actual hours worked from home. Vehicle logbooks must cover a continuous 12-week period and be renewed every five years per TR 2017/D6.

## 3. Cash Economy Businesses

The ATO identifies cash-intensive industries — hospitality, construction, hairdressing, taxi/rideshare — as high-risk. They use benchmarks to compare your declared turnover against industry norms for your location, size, and employee count.

If your restaurant reports 20% less revenue than comparable restaurants in the same suburb, the ATO's analytics will flag it.

**What to do:** Use electronic payment systems wherever possible. Maintain complete records of all cash transactions. If your margins genuinely differ from industry benchmarks, document why (e.g., higher rent, different menu positioning).

## 4. R&D Tax Incentive Claims

The R&D Tax Incentive (administered jointly by the ATO and AusIndustry under Division 355 of the ITAA 1997) remains one of the most heavily audited programmes. The ATO is specifically targeting:

- Software development activities claimed as R&D that are actually routine development
- Claims where the "technical uncertainty" requirement isn't substantiated
- Overseas R&D activities without Overseas Findings from AusIndustry
- Claims where expenditure records don't match the registered R&D activities

The ATO and AusIndustry conducted 280+ compliance reviews of R&D claims in 2024-25 alone.

**What to do:** Maintain contemporaneous records of R&D activities — experiment logs, hypotheses, test results, and evidence of technical uncertainty. Don't retrospectively classify routine work as R&D. Engage a specialist R&D tax adviser before lodging.

## 5. Division 7A Loan Compliance

Division 7A of the ITAA 1936 deems certain payments and loans from private companies to shareholders (or associates) as unfranked dividends unless they comply with specific loan agreement requirements.

The ATO is actively auditing:

- Loans from private companies to shareholders without compliant Division 7A loan agreements
- Minimum yearly repayment obligations not being met
- Loan agreements that don't meet the benchmark interest rate (currently published annually by the ATO, 8.27% for FY2025-26)
- Company payments of shareholder personal expenses

**What to do:** If your private company has made loans to shareholders, ensure each loan has a compliant written agreement, charges at least the benchmark interest rate, and that minimum yearly repayments are made on time. Review annually with your accountant.

## 6. Cryptocurrency and Digital Assets

Since 2020, the ATO has received data from Australian cryptocurrency exchanges under its data-matching programme. They now have visibility of all buy, sell, and transfer transactions.

Common errors include:

- Failing to declare capital gains on crypto disposals (including crypto-to-crypto swaps)
- Treating personal-use crypto as exempt when transaction values exceed $10,000
- Not reporting staking, airdrops, or DeFi yields as assessable income

**What to do:** Track every transaction with a crypto tax tool. Report all disposals as capital gains events. Staking and yield farming income is assessable in the year it's received — not when you sell.

## 7. Omitted Foreign Income

Under the CRS, the ATO automatically receives financial account information from over 100 jurisdictions. If you hold bank accounts, investments, or property overseas and haven't declared the income — the ATO already has the data.

**What to do:** Declare all foreign income including interest, dividends, rental income, and capital gains. Claim foreign income tax offsets where applicable under Division 770 of the ITAA 1997.

## 8. GST Fraud and Overclaiming

The ATO is using advanced analytics to detect GST fraud patterns, particularly:

- Fictitious invoices used to claim input tax credits
- GST-free supplies incorrectly claimed as taxable (to generate refunds)
- Abnormally high GST refunds relative to business size
- Failure to remit GST collected on sales

**What to do:** Maintain valid tax invoices for all GST claims. Reconcile BAS lodgements against your accounting software quarterly. If you're consistently receiving GST refunds, ensure you can substantiate every claim.

## 9. Contractor vs Employee Classification

The ATO continues to scrutinise businesses that classify workers as independent contractors when the working arrangement is actually employment. This affects:

- Superannuation guarantee obligations (currently 11.5%, increasing to 12% from 1 July 2025)
- PAYG withholding obligations
- Workers' compensation and payroll tax

The ATO uses a multi-factor test based on the High Court's decisions in *Personnel Contracting* and *Jamsek* (2022), focusing on the terms of the written contract.

**What to do:** Review all contractor arrangements against the ATO's contractor vs employee decision tool. If in doubt, get a private ruling from the ATO — it's free and binding.

## 10. Late or Non-Lodgement

This one is straightforward. If you haven't lodged your BAS, income tax return, or STP reports on time, the ATO's automated systems will issue penalties and flag your account for review. Late lodgement is the simplest trigger to avoid and the most damaging to ignore.

**What to do:** Lodge on time, every time. If you can't lodge by the due date, contact the ATO before the deadline to arrange an extension — they're generally reasonable if you ask in advance.
