# Division 7A Loans Explained — Avoid the ATO Trap (2026 Guide)

**Legislative references**: ITAA 1936 Division 7A (Sections 109B–109ZB), PS LA 2010/4

---

Division 7A of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1936 is the ATO's primary weapon against shareholders and associates extracting profits from private companies without paying tax. It deems certain payments, loans, and debt forgiveness as unfranked dividends — assessable at the shareholder's marginal tax rate with no franking credits to offset the liability.

The ATO has intensified compliance activity around Division 7A in recent years, particularly targeting unpaid present entitlements from trusts and informal director loan arrangements. If you are a director, shareholder, or associate of a private company, understanding what triggers a deemed dividend — and how to avoid one — is essential.

## What Triggers a Deemed Dividend

Division 7A applies when a private company makes a payment or loan to a shareholder or their associate, and that amount is not dealt with as a dividend, salary, or other assessable payment. The legislation captures four main categories.

### Loans

Any loan from a private company to a shareholder or associate triggers Division 7A unless the loan is on compliant terms (see below). "Loan" is defined broadly under Section 109D and includes formal lending arrangements, informal advances, running account balances, and any situation where a shareholder owes money to the company or the company has provided funds to the shareholder.

### Payments on Behalf of a Shareholder

If the company pays a personal expense of a shareholder or associate — a credit card bill, a holiday, school fees — the payment is a deemed dividend under Section 109C unless it is recorded as salary, wages, or director's fees and taxed accordingly.

### Debt Forgiveness

If the company forgives a debt owed by a shareholder or associate, the forgiven amount is treated as a deemed dividend under Section 109F. This includes informal write-offs where the company simply stops pursuing repayment.

### Use of Company Assets

Where a shareholder or associate uses company assets — a vehicle, a holiday property, equipment — without paying market-rate consideration, the difference between the market value of the use and any amount actually paid is a deemed dividend under Section 109C.

## How to Make a Loan Compliant

Division 7A does not prohibit companies from lending to shareholders. It requires that loans be on specific terms. A compliant loan agreement (often called a "Section 109N agreement") must be in place before the earlier of the due date for lodgement of the company's tax return and the actual lodgement date for the income year in which the loan was made.

### Written Agreement Requirements

The agreement must be written and must specify:

- **Principal amount**: The total amount loaned
- **Term**: Maximum 7 years for unsecured loans, maximum 25 years for loans secured over real property (with a registered mortgage)
- **Interest rate**: At or above the ATO's benchmark interest rate for the income year — currently approximately 8.27% for FY2025-26, published annually by the ATO based on the RBA indicator lending rate for small business
- **Minimum yearly repayments**: Calculated using the formula in Section 109E, which amortises principal and interest over the loan term

### The Minimum Repayment Trap

This is where most Division 7A problems occur. The minimum yearly repayment must be made by 30 June each year. If the minimum repayment is not made in full, the shortfall — or in some cases the entire outstanding loan balance — is treated as a deemed dividend for that income year.

Partial payments do not satisfy the requirement. If the minimum yearly repayment is $28,000 and the shareholder pays $27,500, the shortfall triggers a deemed dividend. There is no rounding, no grace period, and no discretion.

For a $200,000 unsecured 7-year loan at 8.27%, the minimum yearly repayment is approximately $38,400. Miss that payment in a tight cash-flow year and the tax consequence is severe — a $200,000 unfranked deemed dividend assessed at the shareholder's marginal rate. At the top marginal rate of 45% plus Medicare levy, that is approximately $94,000 in unexpected tax.

## Unpaid Present Entitlements — The Most Common Trap

An unpaid present entitlement (UPE) arises when a trust distributes income to a corporate beneficiary, but the company does not actually receive the funds. The trust retains the cash while the distribution sits as a credit in the company's favour on the trust's balance sheet.

Prior to FY2022-23, the ATO generally tolerated UPEs held under "sub-trust" arrangements. That position changed with the finalisation of Practical Statement of Law Administration PS LA 2010/4 (as updated). The ATO now treats UPEs arising from FY2022-23 onwards as loans from the corporate beneficiary to the trust, bringing them within Division 7A.

This affects almost every Australian family group structure where a discretionary trust distributes income to a corporate beneficiary and retains the cash. If you have a family trust distributing to a bucket company and the distributions remain unpaid, you have a Division 7A exposure.

The required action is to place the UPE on compliant Division 7A terms — written agreement, benchmark interest rate, maximum 7-year term, minimum yearly repayments — or to physically pay the distribution to the company before the lodgement day.

## Five Common Mistakes

**1. No written agreement.** Verbal arrangements between a director and their company are not compliant. Even if repayments are being made on schedule, the absence of a written agreement meeting Section 109N requirements means the entire loan balance is a deemed dividend.

**2. Missing minimum repayments.** One missed payment, one short payment, one late payment (after 30 June) — any of these triggers a deemed dividend. Directors in cash-tight years often defer their loan repayment as the "least urgent" obligation. It is the most expensive one to miss.

**3. Ignoring interposed entities.** Section 109T extends Division 7A to indirect arrangements. A loan from Company A to Company B to the shareholder is still caught. The ATO looks through interposed entities to identify the ultimate recipient. Restructuring loan pathways to avoid Division 7A is itself an avoidance arrangement.

**4. Forgetting asset usage.** The director who drives the company car, lives in the company's investment property, or uses company equipment without paying market-rate consideration has a deemed dividend exposure. Sole-director companies are particularly prone to overlooking this.

**5. Ignoring UPEs post-2022.** Transitional rules applied to pre-existing UPEs, but many businesses have not acted. Each income year that passes with a non-compliant UPE creates a fresh deemed dividend exposure. The cost of inaction compounds annually.

## How to Fix an Existing Division 7A Problem

### Retrospective Loan Agreements

If a loan was made without a compliant agreement, one can be executed retrospectively — provided it is put in place before the earlier of the lodgement due date and the actual lodgement date for the income year in which the loan was made. The agreement must meet all Section 109N requirements from that point forward, including minimum yearly repayments calculated from the original advance date.

### Dividend Declarations

An alternative is to declare a franked dividend to the shareholder equal to the loan amount. This effectively "repays" the loan on paper. It works if the company has sufficient franking credits and the shareholder's marginal tax rate makes a franked dividend preferable to a deemed unfranked dividend. This is a common clean-up strategy for smaller amounts.

### Loan Repayment Before Lodgement Day

If the loan is repaid in full before the lodgement day of the company's tax return, Division 7A does not apply. However, beware of the "repayment and re-borrowing" anti-avoidance rule in Section 109R — repaying a loan and then re-borrowing a similar amount shortly after is treated as if the repayment never occurred.

## The Proposed Reforms

Treasury has been consulting on Division 7A simplification since 2018. Proposed changes include a single maximum 10-year loan term (replacing the 7/25 split), a simplified benchmark interest rate calculation, and a self-correction mechanism for inadvertent breaches. As of March 2026, no amending legislation has been introduced. The current rules remain in full force.

Do not plan your compliance strategy around proposed reforms. Comply with the law as it stands.
