Mould in a commercial building is not a cleaning problem. It is a health hazard, a liability risk, and — if handled incorrectly — an escalating structural issue that compounds in cost every week it goes unaddressed.

The IICRC S520 Standard and Reference Guide for Professional Mould Remediation is the internationally recognised protocol for assessing, containing, and remediating mould contamination in commercial and residential structures. In Australia, it is the benchmark that insurers, workplace health and safety regulators, and litigation experts reference when evaluating whether mould remediation was performed correctly.

If you manage a commercial property and mould has been identified, here's what S520 requires — and why cutting corners creates more problems than it solves.

## What the IICRC S520 Standard Mandates

S520 establishes a systematic process for mould remediation. It is not a set of suggestions. It is a defined protocol with measurable criteria at every stage.

### 1. Assessment and Inspection

Before any remediation begins, a qualified assessor must determine the extent of mould contamination. This involves:

- **Visual inspection** of all accessible surfaces, including behind wall cavities, above ceiling tiles, and inside HVAC systems
- **Moisture mapping** to identify the water source driving mould growth — mould doesn't appear without sustained moisture, and remediation without addressing the source is futile
- **Sampling** — Surface swabs, tape lifts, or air sampling to identify mould species and spore concentration levels

S520 distinguishes between Condition 1 (normal fungal ecology — no remediation needed), Condition 2 (settled spores on surfaces — cleaning required), and Condition 3 (active growth with structural contamination — full remediation required). The condition determines the remediation scope.

### 2. Air Quality Testing

Indoor air quality (IAQ) testing is a cornerstone of S520 compliance. Pre-remediation air samples establish baseline spore counts. These are compared against outdoor control samples to determine whether indoor fungal levels are elevated.

Testing must be performed by a qualified assessor — not the remediation contractor. S520 explicitly separates the roles of assessor and remediator to prevent conflicts of interest. The assessor sets the scope and verifies clearance. The remediator executes the work.

For commercial properties, air sampling locations should include the affected area, adjacent unaffected areas, HVAC return air, and at least one outdoor control point.

### 3. Containment Protocol

Containment prevents cross-contamination during remediation. S520 specifies containment requirements based on the contamination size and condition:

- **Limited containment** (Condition 2, small areas): Polyethylene sheeting barriers, HEPA-filtered negative air pressure, dust suppression
- **Full containment** (Condition 3, large areas): Floor-to-ceiling polyethylene enclosures, double-barrier entry/exit, continuous negative air pressure with HEPA filtration, decontamination chamber for workers

In commercial buildings, containment also means isolating HVAC zones to prevent spore distribution through ductwork. This is frequently overlooked in non-compliant remediation and is a primary cause of mould recurrence in multi-tenancy buildings.

### 4. Remediation Methods

S520-compliant remediation uses a combination of:

- **Physical removal** — Contaminated porous materials (plasterboard, carpet, insulation) that cannot be cleaned are removed and disposed of as contaminated waste
- **HEPA vacuuming** — All surfaces within the containment zone are HEPA-vacuumed to capture settled spores
- **Antimicrobial treatment** — Non-porous surfaces are treated with registered antimicrobial agents following manufacturer specifications
- **Structural drying** — Moisture levels are reduced to below the growth threshold for the material type (typically below 16% for timber, below 1% for concrete)

Bleach is not an S520-compliant mould remediation method. It is a surface disinfectant that does not penetrate porous materials, does not address embedded hyphae, and gives a false impression of remediation. If your contractor's plan starts with bleach, find another contractor.

### 5. Clearance Criteria

Remediation is not complete until the independent assessor verifies clearance. S520 clearance requires:

- **Visual confirmation** — No visible mould growth on any surface within the remediation area
- **Moisture verification** — All materials at or below dry standard
- **Post-remediation air sampling** — Indoor spore counts at or below outdoor control levels (Condition 1 restored)

Without clearance testing, there is no evidence that remediation was successful. Insurers will not close claims without it. Tenants retain the right to challenge the building's fitness for occupation.

## When to Call a Professional (Always)

Commercial mould remediation is not a DIY task. Under Safe Work Australia's model Code of Practice for Managing the Work Environment and Facilities, employers have a duty to maintain workplaces free from health hazards. Mould contamination that causes respiratory illness, allergic reactions, or exacerbation of asthma creates a foreseeable risk that triggers WHS obligations.

If a worker or tenant suffers health effects from mould exposure in a building where remediation was performed by uncertified personnel using non-compliant methods, the building owner faces potential liability under WHS legislation, tenancy law, and common law negligence.

There is no scenario in which a commercial property owner should attempt mould remediation without IICRC-certified professionals following S520 protocols. The cost of compliant remediation is a fraction of the cost of litigation, tenant claims, and repeated failed remediation attempts.
