# What Your Insurer Actually Requires from Your Restoration Contractor

**Word count**: ~750
**Target keyword**: "insurance restoration contractor requirements Australia"

When water, fire, or mould damages a commercial property, the restoration contractor you engage doesn't just affect the repair timeline — they directly determine whether your insurance claim is approved, disputed, or denied.

Insurers have specific requirements for restoration work. Most property managers don't know what they are until a claim is knocked back. Here's what major Australian insurers actually look for.

## IICRC Certification Is Not Optional

Every major insurer operating in Australia — IAG, Suncorp, QBE, Allianz — references IICRC certification in their approved contractor criteria. The Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification sets the international standard for water damage restoration, fire and smoke restoration, and mould remediation.

If your contractor isn't IICRC-certified, two things happen. First, the insurer's assessor will question the quality and methodology of the work. Second, if the damage recurs — mould reappears, structural moisture persists — the insurer can argue the original restoration was non-compliant and deny the follow-up claim.

At minimum, look for WRT (Water Damage Restoration Technician) certification. For fire damage, FSRT. For mould, AMRT. These aren't nice-to-haves. They're the credentials your insurer expects to see on the scope documentation.

## Moisture Documentation to IICRC S500

The IICRC S500 Standard and Reference Guide for Professional Water Damage Restoration defines how water damage should be assessed, categorised, and dried. Insurers use S500 as the benchmark for determining whether restoration work was performed correctly.

Your contractor must provide:

- **Initial moisture readings** — Taken at the time of assessment using calibrated moisture meters and thermal imaging. These establish the baseline and determine the damage category (Category 1, 2, or 3) and class (Class 1 through 4).
- **Daily drying logs** — Moisture readings recorded at consistent intervals (typically every 12–24 hours) showing progressive drying across all affected materials.
- **Final clearance readings** — Documented confirmation that moisture levels have returned to the dry standard for the material type, as defined in S500.

Without this documentation trail, the insurer has no evidence that drying was completed properly. Claims stall. Disputes escalate.

## Scope of Works Format

Insurers expect a formal scope of works before restoration begins. This document should include:

- **Affected areas** mapped by room, level, and material type
- **Damage category and class** per the IICRC S500 classification
- **Proposed remediation method** for each affected zone
- **Equipment deployment plan** — number and type of dehumidifiers, air movers, air scrubbers
- **Timeline estimate** with milestones
- **Cost breakdown** by trade and material

A scope of works that reads like a text message — "fix water damage in office, approx $15k" — will be sent back for rework. Every time that happens, the claim timeline extends.

## Photo and Video Evidence

Insurers require photographic evidence at four stages:

1. **Pre-restoration** — Damage as found, before any demolition or intervention
2. **During demolition** — Extent of concealed damage (behind walls, under flooring)
3. **During drying** — Equipment in place, moisture meter readings visible
4. **Post-restoration** — Completed work, final condition

Timestamped photos linked to the scope of works. Insurers increasingly request video walkthroughs as well, particularly for losses exceeding $50,000.

## Report Submission Timelines

Most insurer agreements specify reporting windows:

- **Initial damage report**: within 24–48 hours of first attendance
- **Scope of works**: within 3–5 business days of assessment
- **Progress reports**: weekly for active restorations
- **Final completion report**: within 5 business days of project close

Missing these windows doesn't automatically void a claim, but it creates friction. Assessors deprioritise files that require chasing. Your claim settles later, and your tenant or business loses more revenue to downtime.
