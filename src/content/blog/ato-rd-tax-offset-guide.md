The R&D Tax Incentive is Australia's largest tax concession programme for private business. Over $2.8 billion is claimed annually, yet thousands of eligible businesses — particularly in construction, manufacturing, and agriculture — leave money on the table because they assume "R&D" means laboratories and software.

It doesn't. If your business systematically experiments with new methods, materials, or processes to solve problems where the outcome is genuinely uncertain, you likely qualify. This guide covers eligibility, rates, registration, common mistakes, and how to maximise your claim.

## What Is the R&D Tax Incentive?

The R&D Tax Incentive provides a tax offset for eligible research and development activities. It operates under Division 355 of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (ITAA 1997) and is jointly administered by AusIndustry (which assesses activity eligibility) and the Australian Taxation Office (which assesses expenditure claims).

Critically, this is not a grant. It is a tax offset applied to your income tax return after you register eligible activities with AusIndustry. For eligible businesses, the offset exceeds the standard company tax rate, meaning the government effectively subsidises your R&D expenditure.

### The Two Offset Rates

**Refundable offset (43.5%) — Aggregated turnover under $20 million**

Entities with aggregated turnover below $20 million receive a 43.5% refundable tax offset on eligible R&D expenditure. The standard company tax rate for these entities is 25%, so the incentive provides an 18.5 percentage point premium. Because it is refundable, you receive a cash refund even if your company has no tax liability — making it valuable for early-stage and loss-making businesses.

**Non-refundable offset — Aggregated turnover $20 million to $50 million**

Entities in this bracket receive a non-refundable offset calculated using an intensity-based premium. The offset rate depends on your R&D intensity (R&D expenditure as a proportion of total expenditure). For expenditure up to 2% intensity, the offset is the company tax rate plus 8.5 percentage points. Above 2% intensity, the premium increases to 16.5 percentage points.

Entities with aggregated turnover above $50 million are ineligible.

### The $20,000 Minimum

You must incur at least $20,000 in eligible R&D expenditure during the income year — unless the R&D is conducted by a Registered Research Service Provider (RSP) on your behalf, in which case there is no minimum.

## Who Is Eligible?

### Entity Requirements

To claim the R&D Tax Incentive, your entity must be:

- An R&D entity as defined in Section 355-35 — generally, a company incorporated in Australia or a foreign company with an Australian permanent establishment
- Registered with AusIndustry for R&D activities in the relevant income year
- Not an exempt entity under the ITAA 1997

Trusts and partnerships cannot claim directly, though corporate beneficiaries and corporate partners may be eligible in certain structures.

### Industries That Qualify — Beyond Tech

The most common misconception about the R&D Tax Incentive is that it is restricted to technology companies. It is not. The legislative test is whether activities involve systematic, investigative, and experimental work where the outcome cannot be determined in advance on the basis of current knowledge.

**Construction**: Developing proprietary formwork systems, testing waterproofing membranes beyond manufacturer specifications for Australian conditions, engineering foundation solutions for reactive clay soils, trialling new concrete mix designs for high-temperature environments.

**Manufacturing**: Process optimisation where the outcome is uncertain, developing new tooling or fixtures, trialling alternative raw materials for cost reduction or performance improvement, quality control experimentation.

**Agriculture**: Crop variety trials, soil treatment experimentation, pest and disease management research, precision agriculture system development, water efficiency innovations.

**Professional services**: Building internal software tools with novel functionality, developing new service delivery methodologies, creating proprietary analytical models or algorithms.

The test is not what industry you are in. The test is whether you are conducting experimental activities with genuinely uncertain outcomes.

## Core R&D vs Supporting Activities

Division 355 defines two categories of eligible activities.

### Core R&D Activities (Section 355-25)

These are the experimental activities at the heart of your claim. To qualify, an activity must:

1. Be conducted for the purpose of generating new knowledge — including new knowledge in the form of new or improved materials, products, devices, processes, or services
2. Involve a systematic progression of work based on principles of established science — proceeding from hypothesis to experiment to evaluation
3. Have an outcome that cannot be determined in advance on the basis of current knowledge, information, or experience

The critical word is "experiment." If you already know the outcome before starting, it is not core R&D. Routine engineering, quality control testing with known parameters, and application of established techniques do not qualify.

### Supporting R&D Activities (Section 355-30)

These are activities directly related to core R&D activities. They include data collection, prototype construction, trial production runs, and testing associated with core R&D. Supporting activities must have the dominant purpose of supporting the core R&D.

A common error is claiming supporting activities without linking them to a registered core R&D activity. AusIndustry will reject supporting activity claims that lack a clearly articulated connection to an eligible core activity.

## Registration and Compliance

### Registration Deadlines

You must register your R&D activities with AusIndustry within 10 months after the end of the income year in which the activities were conducted. For a standard 30 June year-end, this means registration must be lodged by 30 April of the following year.

This deadline is strict. There is no discretion for late registration. If you miss it, the entire year's R&D expenditure becomes ineligible.

### Record-Keeping Requirements

AusIndustry and the ATO require contemporaneous records — documentation created at the time the R&D was conducted, not written retrospectively to support a claim. Required records include:

- **Project documentation**: Hypotheses, experimental methodology, variables being tested, results and conclusions
- **Activity logs**: Who conducted the R&D, when, and for how long (for salary apportionment)
- **Financial records**: Expenditure linked to specific R&D activities — wages, contractor costs, materials, depreciation
- **Meeting minutes and correspondence**: Evidence of decision-making around experimental direction

Retrospective reconstruction of R&D records is the single most common reason for claim rejection and a primary ATO audit trigger.

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Not Claiming at All

The most expensive mistake. Many businesses, particularly in construction and manufacturing, conduct activities that meet all legislative criteria but never register because their accountant categorises R&D as a "tech sector" concession. An AI-assisted compliance audit cross-references your activity records against current ATO rulings and identifies eligible work that human reviewers routinely overlook.

### 2. Claiming Routine Activities

The opposite problem. Some businesses claim activities that are systematic and scientific but lack genuine uncertainty. Process improvements where the outcome is predictable, quality control testing within established parameters, and engineering work that applies known solutions to known problems do not qualify. AusIndustry's compliance reviews specifically target over-claiming.

### 3. Poor Activity Descriptions

Registration requires clear, technical descriptions of what was done, why it qualifies as experimental, and what the unknown outcome was. Vague descriptions — "we tested new materials" — trigger compliance reviews. Descriptions must identify the hypothesis, the experimental method, and why the outcome was not determinable in advance.

### 4. Missing the Registration Deadline

As noted above, late registration voids the entire claim. Calendar the deadline. For FY2025-26, registration closes 30 April 2027.

### 5. Failing to Separate Core and Supporting Activities

Every supporting activity must be linked to a core activity. Claiming supporting activities without a registered core activity is a rejection trigger. Structure your registration so that each supporting activity references a specific core activity by name.
